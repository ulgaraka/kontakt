Recenzje i pytania

Preliminary Clinical results based on patient interviews

Optimising therapeutic RSO use can be a challenge – for doctors as well as patients. Most physicians never learned about RSO in medical school and, according to a 2017 survey, few feel they are qualified to counsel patients about dosage and usage of such products. To assist anyone in search for more information, we conducted our own clinical study to investigate the effects of RSO products on various health condition.
The main purpose of this study was to understand the effects of short and long term RSO usage and help to improve future derived products.

The preliminary results were obtained from 135 patients from 8 different European countries that have filled an evaluation form about their use and experiences of RSO products.

Health Conditions researched

Patients who involved in this research had a wide range of health conditions. Related health conditions were combined as one group to allow a better fitting result. As such, all cancer types were combined into one group, this applied also for patients that indicated to use RSO to ease pain due to cancer. Other patients indicated to use RSO for various types of pain relief or chronic pain, therefore, all causes of pain (with the exception of cancer) were combined into another group. Patients that indicated to have suffer from depression, stress and/or anxiety were also placed as one health condition group.

Cancer was found to be the present for the largest part of the patients (26,4% or 29 patients), followed by pain (16,4% or 21 patients) and depression, stress and/or anxiety (14,5% or 16 patients) as seen in figure 1.

Furthermore, 7,3% of the patients used RSO for multiple sclerosis, 6,4% used it for sleeping problems like insomnia, 5,5% used it for fibromyalgia. Other users indicated to have used RSO products for arthritis, epilepsy, diabetes, neuropathy, spinal disorders, migraine, COPD, PTSD, Lyme, Crohn's disease, bronchitis, recreational use, sexual stimulation and more.


Figure 1. Use of RSO products for health conditions or reasons

Effects of usage?

Patients were asked to indicate whether the RSO products that they used for their health conditions did satisfy their expectation.

A staggering amount of 83,9% of the patients indicated that RSO products indeed did satisfy their expectations to use it for their health condition. 12,9% was not sure and 3,2% indicated that it did not met their expectations (see figure 2).


Figure 2. Expectations of RSO products from all patients.

78,6% of the 29 cancer patients that participated in this research indicated that the RSO products satisfied their expectations, while 21,4% did not know if it has done anything (see figure 3).


Figure 3. Expectations of RSO products from cancer patients.

95,7% of the 21 patients with pain, indicated that the RSO products satisfied their expectations, while 4,3% did not know if it has done anything to them (see figure 4).


Figure 4. Expectations of RSO products from patients with pain.

80,0% of the 16 patients, with depression, stress or anxiety, indicated that the RSO products satisfied their expectations, while 13,3% did not know if it has done anything to them and 6,7% said it has not met their satisfaction (see figure 5).


Figure 5. Expectations of RSO products from patients with depression, stress or anxiety.

RSO product type usage

There are different type of RSO products available with different strengths. Two active components of RSO are widely used for their benefits. Both types are used for different conditions. Type I component is classified as a low to intermediate effect and used for stress, sleep, epilepsy, Alzheimer, PTSD, MS and more, while the type II component is classified as a strong RSO effect and often used with more severe conditions, to relief pain and chemo relief.

41,6% of the patients used a RSO product that contained both active components, 25,5% of the patients used mainly products with the type I component (low to intermediate effect) and 30,7% used a product with mainly the type II component (strong effect). 2,2% was not sure what RSO product they had used (see figure 6).


Figure 6. RSO used by all patients. Type I component is classified as a strong RSO effect. Type II component is classified as a low to intermediate RSO effect. Both types are used for different conditions.

45,2% of the cancer patients used a RSO product that contained both active components. 12,9% of these patients used mainly products with the type I and 38,7% used a product with mainly the type II component. 3,6% was not sure what RSO product they had used (see figure 7).


Figure 7. RSO used by patients with cancer.

44,4% of the patients, that mentioned to use it for pain relief, used a RSO product that contained both active components. 22,2% of these patients used mainly products with type I and 33,3% used a product with mainly type II (see figure 8).


Figure 8. RSO used by patients with pain.

47,1% of the 16 patients, that mentioned to use it for either depression, stress relief or anxiety, used a RSO product that contained active components. 29,4% of these patients used mainly products with type I component and 23,5% used a product with mainly the type II component (see figure 9).


Figure 9. RSO used by patients with stress/depression/anxiety.

A recent 2020 study found that RSO improves blood flow to parts of the brain associated with memory and decision-making. MRI scans showed type II increased blood flow to the hippocampus, responsible for memories and the orbitofrontal cortex, responsible for decision-making. Researchers say the findings could be an important discovery for conditions which affect memory, such as Alzheimer's disease and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and could help better target therapies.

Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSO) Effect of oil palm sustainability certification on deforestation and fire in Indonesia (current status 2021).Many major corporations and countries have made commitments to purchase or produce only “sustainable” palm oil, a commodity responsible for substantial tropical forest loss. Sustainability certification is the tool most used to fulfill these procurement policies, and around 20% of global palm oil production was certified by the Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSO) in 2017. However, the effect of certification on deforestation in oil palm plantations remains unclear. Here, we use a comprehensive dataset of RSO certified and non-certified oil palm plantations (∼188,000 km2) in Indonesia, the leading producer of palm oil, as well as annual remotely sensed metrics of tree cover loss and fire occurrence, to evaluate the impact of certification on deforestation and fire from 2001 to 2015. While forest loss and fire continued after RSPO certification, certified palm oil was associated with reduced deforestation. Certification lowered deforestation by 33% from a counterfactual of 9.8 to 6.6% y−1. Nevertheless, most plantations contained little residual forest when they received certification. As a result, by 2015, certified areas held less than 1% of forests remaining within Indonesian oil palm plantations. Moreover, certification had no causal impact on forest loss in peatlands or active fire detection rates. Broader adoption of certification in forested regions, strict requirements to avoid all peat, and routine monitoring of clearly defined forest cover loss in certified and RSPO member-held plantations appear necessary if the RSPO is to yield conservation and climate benefits from reductions in tropical deforestation.
